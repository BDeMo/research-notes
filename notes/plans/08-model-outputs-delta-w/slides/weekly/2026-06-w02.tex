\begin{frame}{Janus (Plan 09): Measurement, Falsification, Surviving Finding}
  \small
  \begin{block}{Question}
    Are the transformer-intrinsic sites that carry long-context behavior the same
    sites that fine-tuning perturbs most?
  \end{block}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Instrument}: 39 intrinsic metrics / 12 angles; 41-cell grid
      (4 model families $\times$ 12 benchmarks), native bf16.
    \item \textbf{First read}: a uniformly positive per-head coupling (looked promising).
    \item \textbf{Reckoning}: under controls (partial-out activation magnitude,
      within-layer, per-model, CIs) the \textbf{head-level coupling dissolves} ---
      the ``two frontiers, one site'' thesis does \emph{not} hold at the head level.
    \item \textbf{Surviving finding}: long-context heads are \textbf{gradient-shielded}
      --- SFT gradients \emph{avoid} long-reach / sink heads (within-layer
      $\rho \approx -0.5\ldots-0.6$).
  \end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Plan 08: Bit-Capacity Wall of Soft-Prompt Memory}
  \small
  \begin{block}{Paper (target ICLR 2027)}
    \emph{Bit-Capacity Limits of Soft-Prompt Memory} --- what a learned, fixed-size
    memory wrapper around a frozen LLM can and cannot encode about long context.
  \end{block}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{(i) A sharp wall}: exact recall $\approx 1.0$ at $\le 2$-bit answer
      entropy, collapsing to $0$ above $\sim 10$ bits; the wrapper and a matched-$K$
      Gist baseline hit the wall \emph{at the same place}.
    \item \textbf{Reading}: the wall is a property of the soft-prompt \emph{read}
      interface, not the write architecture.
    \item \textbf{(ii) Below the wall} the wrapper works but not robustly (seed-sensitive;
      improves with more synthetic data).
    \item This generalizes the earlier ``three-regime'' characterization. Repo:
      \texttt{latent-mem-paper}; facts:
      \href{https://github.com/BDeMo/research-notes/blob/main/notes/plans/08-model-outputs-delta-w/v1-results-2026-06-03.md}{v1 results}.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{dLLM Best-of-N: Where Should a Verifier Read?}
  \small
  \begin{block}{Paper (submitted to EMNLP 2026)}
    Best-of-$N$ selection verifiers for diffusion language models.
  \end{block}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item AR decoder-only verifiers read the \emph{final} token because of
      \textbf{confluence}: under causal masking, that position has seen the whole
      prompt and answer.
    \item Diffusion LLMs are bidirectional and fill a masked canvas, so the inherited
      final position may be padding / an end marker --- \textbf{confluence is broken}.
    \item \textbf{Fix}: read the \emph{output-span} (answer-bearing region), not the
      inherited tail. On LLaDA-8B / GSM8K this lifts a linear verifier
      $75.4 \to 83.7$; on the LLaDA2.0-mini MoE the boundary flips.
    \item No universal rule --- match readout to generation geometry, check linear
      separability, separate verifier error from candidate-set limits. Connects to
      Plan 03 (W-space Best-of-$N$) and the AR$\to$dLLM design rule.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
